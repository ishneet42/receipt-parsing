%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
%	MOTIVATION
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\headerbox{Motivation \& Problem}{name=motivation,column=0,row=0, span=2}{

\textbf{Bill-splitting apps (Splitwise, Venmo) still require users to manually type itemized
amounts.} A receipt-photographed pipeline that automatically extracts every line item
and price would close that gap — but requires \emph{item-level} extraction, not just
the document-level fields most receipt benchmarks emphasize.

\medskip

Two competing architectural paradigms exist:
\begin{itemize}\compresslist
\item \textbf{OCR-dependent:} an external OCR engine produces tokens + boxes; a multimodal
transformer classifies each token. Risks \emph{error propagation} from OCR mistakes.
\item \textbf{OCR-free:} an end-to-end transformer maps raw pixels to structured output,
with no OCR step. Risks \emph{hallucination} but bypasses OCR errors entirely.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research questions:}
\begin{enumerate}\compresslist
\item Which paradigm is better at item-level extraction on a clean benchmark (CORD)?
\item How much of the OCR-dependent system's apparent score is the model vs.\ the OCR?
\item Can the two be \emph{composed} to inherit each other's strengths?
\end{enumerate}
}

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
%	METHODS
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\headerbox{Methods}{name=methods,column=2,row=0, span=2}{

\textbf{Models compared}
\begin{itemize}\compresslist
\item \textbf{LayoutLMv3} (Microsoft, OCR-dependent): Tesseract v5 $\rightarrow$
multimodal transformer $\rightarrow$ token classification with \textbf{BIO labels} over 5
target fields. Fine-tuned on CORD v2 ourselves on a MacBook Air (MPS).
\item \textbf{Donut} (Naver, OCR-free): Swin Transformer encoder + BART decoder
generates JSON directly. We use \texttt{naver-clova-ix/donut-base-finetuned-cord-v2}.
\item \textbf{Hybrid (ours):} field-routed composition — line items
$\leftarrow$ LayoutLMv3, aggregates $\leftarrow$ Donut. No additional training.
\end{itemize}

\medskip

\textbf{Target fields (CORD):} \texttt{menu.nm}, \texttt{menu.price}, \texttt{menu.cnt},
\texttt{sub\_total.subtotal\_price}, \texttt{total.total\_price} \\
\textbf{Test set:} 100 CORD test receipts. \textbf{Tracking:} Weights \& Biases.

\medskip

\textbf{Metrics:}
\begin{itemize}\compresslist
\item \textit{Entity-level F1} per field (multiset matching, exact + normalized)
\item \textit{Micro-F1} over all 5 fields
\item \textit{Line-item matching accuracy} — fraction of (name, price) pairs jointly correct
\item \textit{Checksum consistency} (novel): does $\sum$items $=$ total within $\pm 5\%$?
\end{itemize}
}

\headerbox{Headline Results — CORD Test (n = 100)}{name=results,column=0, below=motivation, span=2}{

\textbf{Per-field F1 (normalized matching):} the two paradigms exhibit a
\emph{complementary strength pattern.}

\medskip

\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Field} & \textbf{LayoutLMv3} & \textbf{Donut} & \textbf{Hybrid} \\
\midrule
\rowcolor{wingreen}\texttt{menu.nm}                  & \textbf{0.899} & 0.791 & \textbf{0.899} \\
\rowcolor{wingreen}\texttt{menu.price}               & \textbf{0.968} & 0.866 & \textbf{0.968} \\
\rowcolor{wingreen}\texttt{menu.cnt}                 & \textbf{0.980} & 0.968 & \textbf{0.980} \\
\rowcolor{lightgray}\texttt{sub\_total.subtotal\_price} & 0.882 & \textbf{0.901} & \textbf{0.901} \\
\rowcolor{lightgray}\texttt{total.total\_price}       & 0.898 & \textbf{0.917} & \textbf{0.917} \\
\midrule
\textbf{Micro F1}                & 0.937 & 0.878 & \textbf{0.940} \\
\textbf{Line-item matching}      & 0.886 & 0.732 & \textbf{0.886} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip

\raggedright
\textbf{Findings:}
\begin{itemize}\compresslist
\item LayoutLMv3 wins all per-item fields (\textcolor{green!50!black}{green rows}); Donut wins both aggregates (\textcolor{gray!70!black}{gray rows}).
\item The \textbf{hybrid strictly beats both single models} (0.940 micro-F1) without retraining.
\item The two paradigms fail in \emph{uncorrelated} ways — exactly what makes composition useful.
\end{itemize}
}

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
%	OCR-SOURCE ABLATION (the bombshell)
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\headerbox{OCR-Source Ablation: How Much Was the OCR Doing?}{name=ocr_ablation,column=2, below=methods, span=2}{

The headline LayoutLMv3 score uses \textbf{CORD's human-verified word boxes} as input.
This conflates ``how good is the classifier?'' with ``how good is the
OCR$\rightarrow$classifier pipeline?'' We swap in real Tesseract OCR — same model, same
images, same metrics — and measure the drop.

\medskip

\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{System} & \textbf{Micro F1} & \textbf{Line-item} & \textbf{total F1} \\
\midrule
LayoutLMv3 + \emph{gold} OCR (CORD) & \textbf{0.937} & \textbf{0.886} & 0.898 \\
\rowcolor{lossred}LayoutLMv3 + Tesseract           & 0.314 & 0.061 & 0.260 \\
Donut (no OCR)                      & 0.878 & 0.732 & \textbf{0.917} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip

\raggedright
\textbf{Findings:}
\begin{itemize}\compresslist
\item Replacing gold OCR with Tesseract drops LayoutLMv3 by \textbf{0.62 absolute micro-F1}.
\item \textbf{Roughly 2/3 of the headline 0.937 score was contributed by the OCR}, not the model.
\item Once Tesseract is substituted in, \textbf{Donut wins by 0.564 micro-F1} on the same images.
\item \textbf{Standard CORD evaluations of OCR-dependent models are upper bounds on deployable performance.}
\end{itemize}
}

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
%	HYBRID + ROUTING ABLATION
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\headerbox{Hybrid Composition \& Routing Ablation}{name=hybrid,column=0, below=results, span=2}{

\begin{minipage}{0.55\textwidth}
\textbf{Why does the hybrid help?} Two questions:
\begin{enumerate}\compresslist
\item Does it beat both single models? \textbf{Yes (0.940 vs 0.937 / 0.878).}
\item Is the gain from \emph{routing direction} or just \emph{ensembling}?
\end{enumerate}

We test (2) with a \textbf{negative control}: a \emph{reversed} hybrid that routes
fields to the model that loses each one (items $\leftarrow$ Donut, aggregates
$\leftarrow$ LayoutLMv3). If ensembling alone helped, the reversed hybrid would also
beat both. It does not.

\medskip

\textbf{Conclusion:} the hybrid's gain comes from the \emph{architecturally correct
routing direction}, not from the act of combining two models.
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}{0.42\textwidth}
\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
\textbf{System} & \textbf{Micro F1} \\
\midrule
LayoutLMv3 (alone)        & 0.937 \\
Donut (alone)             & 0.878 \\
\rowcolor{wingreen}\textbf{Hybrid (ours)}            & \textbf{0.940} \\
\rowcolor{lossred}Hybrid (\emph{reversed})  & 0.874 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{minipage}
}

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
%	LABEL CONSTRUCTION ABLATION + CHECKSUM
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\headerbox{Label-Construction Ablation \& Checksum Consistency}{name=is_key,column=2, below=ocr_ablation, span=2}{

\begin{minipage}{0.5\textwidth}
\textbf{The \texttt{is\_key} bug fix.} CORD marks key tokens (e.g., the word
\texttt{TOTAL} preceding the value) with an \texttt{is\_key=1} flag. Our initial
training script labeled both keys and values with the same BIO target, causing the
model to emit ``\texttt{TOTAL 60.000}'' instead of ``\texttt{60.000}''. Filtering
\texttt{is\_key=1} tokens to background \textbf{adds +0.150 micro-F1} (concentrated
in aggregate fields).

\smallskip
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Variant}            & \textbf{Micro} & \textbf{subtotal} & \textbf{total} \\
\midrule
no \texttt{is\_key} filter  & 0.787 & 0.074 & 0.010 \\
\rowcolor{wingreen}with \texttt{is\_key} filter & \textbf{0.937} & \textbf{0.882} & \textbf{0.898} \\
\midrule
$\Delta$                    & \textbf{+0.150} & \textbf{+0.808} & \textbf{+0.888} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}{0.46\textwidth}
\textbf{Checksum consistency.} Does $\sum$items $=$ total $\pm 5\%$? Even CORD's own
ground truth only passes $\sim$53\% (real receipts have tax/fees). \textbf{All systems
sit at this ceiling}, so pass-rate alone doesn't discriminate. The
\emph{mean absolute error} does:

\smallskip
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
\textbf{System} & \textbf{Pass} & \textbf{Mean err} \\
\midrule
Gold ceiling  & 0.532 & 6.0\% \\
\rowcolor{wingreen}LayoutLMv3 & 0.549 & \textbf{5.9\%} \\
\rowcolor{lossred}Donut      & 0.536 & \textbf{22{,}691\%} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\smallskip
\raggedright
Donut occasionally hallucinates totals \textbf{four orders of magnitude wrong} — a
failure mode invisible to F1 but catastrophic for bill-splitting.
\end{minipage}
}

\headerbox{Real-World Test: Photographed Grocery Receipt}{name=realworld,column=0, below=hybrid, span=2}{

We photographed two real US grocery-store receipts and ran both pipelines end-to-end.

\medskip

\begin{minipage}{0.5\textwidth}
\textbf{\texttt{bill.png} — Kroger long receipt:}
\begin{itemize}\compresslist
\item \textbf{LayoutLMv3 + Tesseract:} Tesseract reads ``TOTAL'' as
\texttt{TOVAL}, \texttt{ioial}; output is unusable nonsense
(\texttt{Pperrevecceresreernesenersneerennienny}, etc.).
\item \textbf{Donut:} recognizable items — \texttt{SAGE TOLLYCHIRT} (Tortellini),
\texttt{ROMANE TEATCUE} (Romaine Lettuce), \texttt{KOROGER EGOS} (Kroger Eggs) —
with plausible prices (\$10.99, \$1.58, \$0.98, \$3.79, \$47.73, ...).
\end{itemize}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}{0.45\textwidth}
\textbf{\texttt{bill2.png} — India Bazaar receipt:}
\begin{itemize}\compresslist
\item LayoutLMv3 + Tesseract extracted 5 prices: \$8.99, \$2.99, \$1.75, \$4.99, \$15.99.
\item \textbf{Sum = \$34.71 = predicted subtotal exactly} — checksum verified.
\item Both models agreed on subtotal (\$34.71) and total (\$36.05).
\item Cross-architecture agreement = a free verification signal.
\end{itemize}
\end{minipage}

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
%	CONCLUSIONS
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\headerbox{Conclusions \& Implications}{name=conclusions,column=2, below=is_key, span=2, above=bottom}{

\textbf{1. Receipt parsing is a composite task.} Line items reward dense token classification
(LayoutLMv3); aggregates reward schema-constrained generation (Donut). Each architecture's
inductive bias matches a different sub-task.

\smallskip

\textbf{2. The hybrid is principled, not ad-hoc.} The reversed-routing negative control
(0.874, below either single model) shows the gain is from \emph{architecturally correct}
routing, not ensembling. Cross-architecture agreement is a free verification signal at
inference time.

\smallskip

\textbf{3. The OCR-source ablation reframes the literature.} Replacing gold CORD OCR
with Tesseract collapses LayoutLMv3 from 0.937 to 0.314 micro-F1 (Donut: 0.878
unchanged). \textbf{Reported CORD scores for OCR-dependent models substantially overstate
deployable performance.}

\smallskip

\textbf{For a real bill-splitting app:} use the hybrid; verify with the checksum;
prefer Donut when image quality is unknown.
